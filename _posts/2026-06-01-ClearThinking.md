# Chapter 1

## 1.1
- Rationality is wasted if you don't know when to use (control your defaults)
- We tend to be more influenced by pathos than logos (*Emotion*)
- Self preservation is behind the instinct to react to things that threaten our self-worth (*Ego*)
- We tend to conform to the norms of the society around us (*Social*)
- We tend to form habits ans seek comfort (*Inertia*)

## 1.2
- Emotions can multiply all of your progress by zero
- Acting instinctively on emotions can have worse, unforseen consequences

## 1.3
- Ego can turn unearned knowledge into reckless confidence
- Unearned confidence may lead to taking poor risks - it rushes us to judgement
- Confidence doesn't change the likelihoods of the good and bad outcomes - it just blinds us to the risk
- *The person who wants to be seen as great shows the world how to manipulate them*
- Our desire to *feel* right overpowers our desire to *be* right. This can lead to arranging the world in arbitrary hierarchies to maintain beliefs

## 1.4
- The social rewards for going with the crowd are felt long before the benefits of going against it are gained
- Don't mistake the comfort of collective actions to signify the validity of those actions for desired results
- Success requires shamelessness
- *Best practices aren't always the best. By definition, they're average.*
- Change happens only when you're willing to think independently, when you do what nobody else is doing, and risk looking like a fool

## 1.5
- Tendency to stay in the comfort zone, rely on old techniques or standards even when they're no longer optimal
- "zone of average" - things are working well enough that we don't feel the need to make any changes
- Public statements can create inertia. Putting something on the record establishes expectations along with social pressure to meet those expectations.

## 1.6
- You unconsciously adopt the habits of the people you spend time with
- The people with the best defaults are typically the people with the best environment


# Chapter 2

## 2.1
- There is always an action you can control, however tiny, that helps you achieve progress
- *kind* is not equal to *nice*. Kind people will tell you things that nice people will not.
- No one cares about your excuses except you
- Exceptional people know they can't change they hand they've been dealt and don't waste time wishing for a better one. They focus on how they can progress with what they have.
- Self accountability: even though you don't control everything, you do control how you respond to everything

## 2.2
- Understanding what you do and don't know is the key to playing games you can win
- Know where you are vulnerable to your defaults

## 2.3
- Self-control - reason before instincts
- Emotions are inanimate objects that don't determine what you do unless you let them. You have the power to decide how to respond to them.
- Inspiration and excitement may get you started, but persistence and routine will keep you going until the goal is reached.

## 2.4
- Self-confidence - humility, being aware of your strengths *and* your weaknesses
- In order to be right, you must be willing to change your mind - face reality
- Outcome over ego - focus on *what's* right instead of *who's* right

## 2.5
- Rules create rituals that have inertia
- Example: think about important decisions for at least one day before deciding - short term discomfort, but massive long term benefits

## 2.6
- We unconsciously become what we're near
- ***Champions don't create the standards of excellence. The standards of excellence create champions.***

## 2.7
- Exemplars - unless you don't have a ruler, you can't straighten what is crooked
- ***Make your own personal board of directors.***


# Chapter 3

## 3.1
- Good choices repeated make time your friend, bad ones make it your enemy
- Bad habits are formed when there is a delay between the action and its consequences
- Few small errors consistently repeated leads to failure
- Managing inbuilt weaknesses - strength + safeguards
- Managing acquired weaknesses - strength

## 3.2
- Prevention - avoid decision making in unfavourable conditions
- Rules - help automate behaviour, help avoid being worn out by constant devision making
- Guard rails - ask yourself what you're trying to accomplish and whether your actions will aid or deter that
- Shift your frame of reference

## 3.3
- Mistakes are a choice - update ideas or ignore failures. Ignoring failures is often more expensive than the first mistake itself.
- Handling mistakes: (i) Accept responsibility, (ii) Learn from the mistake, (iii) Commit to doing better, and (iV) Repair the damage as best you can
- What you tell yourself has the power to anchor you to the past or to move you forward


# Chapter 4

## 4.1
- The most critical step in decision making is to get the problem right. We tend to focus more on solving a problem rather than on which problem it is that we're solving.
- Root cause: what would have to be true for this problem not to exist in the first place?
- Separate *problem defining* from *problem solving*

## 4.2
- ***You must never confuse faith that you will prevail in the end - which you cannot afford to lose, with the discipline to confront the most brutal facts of your current reality, whatever they might be.***
- Premortem - fortify and prepare for any problems that may come up. Don't just imagine the ideal future outcome. Imagine the things that could go wrong and how you'll overcome them if they do.
- First order thinking - short term, Second order thinking - long term implications
- Force yourself to explore at least 3 possible solutions to a problem - if you find only 2, force yourself to find at least one more
- When faced with binary choices, try to combine them!
- Hidden tradeoffs - opportunity costs

## 4.3
- Features of criteria for choosing solution: Clarity, Goal promotion, Decisiveness
- Identify and focus on the important thing
- High fidelity - info from source without filters and abstractions
- Evaluate the motives and incentives of your sources
- Get high expertise information - understand how an expert thinks, what variables they consider relevant.

## 4.4
- If the cost to undo a decision is low (high), make it as soon (late) as possible.

## 4.5
- Always have a margin of safety (depends on consequentiality), do not fall in the trap of the defaults
- Bullets before cannonballs
- Live with a devision before announcing it - examine your thinking/logic


# Chapter 5

## 5.1
- The quality of the things we pursue determines the quality of your life
- Happiness isn't conditional
- Wisdom - knowing how to get what you want *and* knowing what is worth wanting

## 5.2
- Life is short - prioritize the things that really matter.
- Worrying wastes your life
- Happiness requires a conscious shift in outlook, in which one chooses daily - optimism over pessimism, hope over despair

## 5.3
- Turn future hindsight into current foresight
- We regret the things we didn't do more than the things we did
- Posessions become less important for what they are than for what they enable
- In the end, everyday moments matter more than the big prizes

## 5.4
- When you know your destination, how to get there becomes clearer - start with the end in mind
- Sometimes the cost of being wise is that other people treat you like a fool
- Develop good judgement: What do I want in life? Is what I want actually worth wanting?
